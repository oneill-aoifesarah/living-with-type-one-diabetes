Diabetes  

  • Type 1 Diabetes is a chronic [life-long] autoimmune disease that prevents your pancreas from making insulin.
  • Daily injections of insulin are needed to keep your blood glucose levels under control.
  • Checking your blood glucose is quick and easy. This can be done by using a blood glucose monitor.
  • Guidelines suggest that you check your blood glucose regularly, including the beginning and end of your day.The ideal blood glucose levels are between 5 to 9 mmol.

Healthy Eating  

  • Knowing how many carbohydrates you're eating and drinking helps you manage your blood glucose levels by matching your fast-acting insulin dose to the amount of carbs in your meals and snacks.
  • Carbohydrates include starchy foods; dairy; sugar; fruits; and honey.
  • Keeping a food diary is useful in learning to track your carbohydrates can be beneficial to ensuring you're eating well and avoiding spikes in your blood glucose levels.
  • Many vegetables, such as carrots, peas, broccoli, and spinach, have a low glycemic index. This means they have a low carbohydrate count and have a minimum impact on blood glucose levels.

Exercise  

  • Regular exercise can help you manage your weight, improve your mood, and contribute to better sleep.
  • However, strenuous activity can cause your blood sugar to drop and lead to low blood sugar, which is called hypoglycemia.
  • Intense bouts of exercise can also cause your blood sugar to rise. If it rises above normal levels, it's known as hyperglycemia.
  • It's important to check your blood sugar before and after periods of exercise. It's also useful to keep a snack close to hand in the event you experience a drop in your blood sugars.
